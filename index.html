The Great Wave off Kanagawa

Written by Marin Lyons

Katsushika Hokusai was influential in the ukiyo-e sphere - ukiyo-e developed with the growth of Japan’s cities and commercial culture during the Edo period. Before, art and paintings in general were produced primarily for aristocratic or warrior patrons, but woodblock prints were made to be reproduced and sold to a broad public audience , with ukiyo-e meaning “pictures of the floating world.” With a growing merchant class, it contributed to new forms of media and popular culture, including kabuki theater, entertainment districts, literature, and commercial art. Earlier ukiyo-e artists frequently depicted actors and courtesans in their work, but Hokusai expanded the genre by establishing a natural landscape as the focus and important subject.

The Great Wave off Kanagawa is a polychrome woodblock print created around 1826–1833. The print was produced with ink and color on paper, and the woodblock measures approximately 25.7 by 37.9 centimeters, although dimensions vary among surviving impressions in which one is held also at the Met Museum. The work shows three boats underneath an enormous breaking wave, with Fujisan appearing in the distance. The wave dominates the foreground and curves over the boats, with the foam separating into smaller claw-like forms that appear to reach toward the fishermen, creating tension and movement that combines careful observation with visual exaggeration. Fujisan is small and stable in the distance and visually contrasts with the temporary violence in the foreground. The triangular form and the curve of both the waves and Fujisan is similar and visually connects the permanent mountain with the changing natural environment. Hokusai’s use of perspective, notably with Fujisan in the backdrop, demonstrates the influence of Western techniques entering Japan through Dutch trade. The large foreground wave and distant appearance of Mount Fuji create a strong sense of depth, while the flattened shapes, bold outlines, and organized patterns remain connected to Japanese woodblock traditions. Prussian blue was recently introduced as a synthetic pigment that Hokusai used, and shows the incorporation of foreign materials into established artistic practices of ukiyo-e. However, two authors challenge the common belief that the image represents a tsunami, which most people believe it is. They argue that it more likely depicts an exceptionally large storm wave, possibly similar to a modern rogue wave through analysis on location, boats, weather conditions, surrounding waves, and physical behavior of breaking water. The authors also identify the wave as a plunging breaker and argue that Hokusai may have closely and accurately observed important characteristics of ocean movement before the invention of modern photography, emphasizing only essential structures over every visible detail.
